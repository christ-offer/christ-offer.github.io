<h2 style="font-size:24px; text-align:center;">What is Codex?</h2>

<br>
<br>

Codex is essentially GPT3 trained on code. As seen below:

<br>
<br>

<div style="text-align:center">
<img src="/img/codex.png" alt="fox in a box" width="600" />
</div>

<br>
<br>

And this is where some of the controversy comes to the forefront. Since the model is trained on open repositories on github, one side of the argument is that OpenAI is "stealing" knowledge and putting it behind a paywall.
An argument that is worth exploring as I do agree models like this should be public resources, paid for by taxes.

<br>

As it stands today tho, sadly it's in the hands of private companies.<br><br>
One of the other arguments is that the model is flawed, and wont be able to do much on it’s own. And that’s a fair argument. It is only as good as the prompt it is given. So in that respect, it is limited.<br><br>

But it’s also very clear that this is just the beginning. And that this model is a stepping stone towards building more powerful AI’s.

<br>
<br>

<h2 style="font-size:18px; text-align:center;">So what’s next?</h2>

<br>
<br>

Well, I think we’re only beginning to scratch the surface. I think it’s only a matter of time before we see AI’s that can not only write code, but also perfectly debug it, write comments and answer complicated code questions.
<br><br>
But all of this is in the future. And as amazing as it all sounds, it’s important to keep in mind that this is still just AI. It’s not human. And it’s not perfect.
<br><br>
So even though it’s amazing that we can now train AI’s to write code, we need to keep in mind that AI’s are still just machines. And as such, they’re subject to the same limitations as any other machine.
<br><br>
They can break. They can make mistakes. And they can fail.
<br>
But that’s ok. Because that’s just part of the journey.
